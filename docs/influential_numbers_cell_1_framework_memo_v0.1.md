## 2. Purpose

Candidate C established, under locked binary decision rules, that 12 beat 10 on the frozen pullback × pooled Phase 3b substrate. That is a statement about one number versus one comparator. It is silent on the shape of the surface in which 12 sits.

The Influential Numbers program asks a structurally different family of questions: whether numbers behave as if influenced by their neighbors rather than standing as isolated privileged points. Cell 1 takes the narrowest, most source-faithful slice of that family — linear-neighborhood influence — and asks whether the local surface around a focal bucket count is structured rather than isolated.

The purpose of this memo is to fix the conceptual boundaries (source vs. extension, umbrella vs. cell, Layer 1 vs. Layer 2, independence from Candidate C, anti-rescue) before any design memo can constrain them under outcome pressure.

## 3. Source attribution and source boundary

**Source.** Kryon channeling, *"Needed Science for the Times,"* channeled by Lee Carroll, delivered in Albuquerque, NM on February 14, 2010; published April 2010 at spiritlibrary.com and originally at kryon.com. The relevant passage appears in the section titled *"The New Math is coming."*

**Canonical example.** "Four is not a four" — four is modified by the numbers that sit next to it, with five and three named as the linear neighbors that influence four.

**What the source directly supports.** Numbers are influenced by adjacent numbers. The named relationship is linear and local: a number and the integers immediately beside it. This — and only this — is the Layer 1 operationalization.

**What the source does not state.**

- It does not state divisor or multiple influence.
- It does not state 12-family, harmonic-family, or duodecimal-family structure.
- It does not single out 12, or any focal number, as special.
- It does not specify a window width, a metric, or a notion of "structure."

**Boundary statement.** Any 12-centered framing in this program originates from Candidate C's result and our researcher-side extension. It does not originate from, and must not borrow authority from, the Kryon source. The source authorizes neighborhood influence as a concept; it authorizes nothing about which number, how wide a neighborhood, or what counts as influence.

## 4. Umbrella program vs. first cell

**Influential Numbers is the umbrella program, not the name of this test.** Cell 1 must not consume the program namespace.

The umbrella admits multiple distinct operationalizations of "a number is influenced by other numbers." Cell 1 deliberately occupies only the one closest to the source.

**Cell 1 — Neighborhood Influence Test:** linear-integer adjacency influence around a focal bucket count.

**Reserved, not designed here, future cells may include:**

- divisor / multiple influence
- harmonic-family influence
- 12-family or duodecimal-family influence
- recursive or weighted neighbor influence
- broader bucket-count surface / smoothness tests

Naming these reserves the namespace honestly. It does not motivate, design, or pre-justify any of them. Each future cell, if pursued, requires its own framework and design treatment.

---

## 5. Layer 1 / Layer 2 distinction

**Layer 1 — linear-neighborhood influence.** Source-faithful. Tested first because it is the closest operationalization to what Kryon directly framed: a number affected by the integers next to it.

**Layer 2 — extensions.** Divisors, multiples, 12-family / duodecimal structure, harmonic families, recursive number fields, weighted neighbor influence. These are researcher-side extensions. They are not directly stated in the source and must not claim source authority.

**Relationship.**

- A positive Layer 1 verdict makes Layer 2 cells more interesting as follow-ups: there would be a real neighborhood effect worth extending.
- A null Layer 1 verdict makes Layer 2 cells less motivated on this substrate: the source-faithful operationalization found nothing to extend.

**Anti-rescue (stated here, restated in §10).** Whether to run Layer 2 at all is a separate, post-Cell-1 decision. Layer 2 must never function as a rescue for a null Layer 1. If Layer 1 returns null and Layer 2 is still contemplated, it requires a separate decision memo explaining why Layer 2 remains scientifically justified despite the Layer 1 null — not because of a desire to find something.

## 6. Relationship to Candidate C

Candidate C asked: **"Does 12 beat 10?"** — and answered, under its locked decision rules, that it did.

Cell 1 asks a different question: **"Does 12 sit inside a structured local neighborhood, or is it an isolated winner?"**

These are independent:

- A **null** Cell 1 result does **not** weaken Candidate C. Candidate C's claim is fully contained in its own locked binary comparison.
- A **positive** Cell 1 result does **not** retroactively reinterpret Candidate C. It does not convert "12 beat 10" into "12 is the center of a base-12 structure."

Candidate C remains exactly: *12 beat 10 under its locked decision rules.*

Cell 1, if run, would add a new, separate atlas tile: whether the surface around 12 behaves as a structured neighborhood, or whether 12 is an isolated point that happened to beat one comparator. It neither inherits nor confers authority across that boundary.

## 7. Substrate posture

**Framework-level default (not yet locked):** the same frozen substrate used by Candidates B and C — pullback × pooled Phase 3b, SPY/EFA/EEM/GLD/TLT, 1,282 trades, 2005–2022.

**Why this is the conservative, audit-compatible default:**

- It is already frozen.
- It is already audited.
- Its structure is already characterized — including its known phase-allocation structure, so Cell 1 would inherit a documented substrate rather than an unexamined one.
- It avoids introducing a new substrate before the concept of neighborhood influence is even clarified, which would confound "is there neighborhood structure?" with "what does this new substrate do?"

**Boundary.** Substrate selection is nonetheless a framework-level decision that remains open. Naming the frozen substrate as the conservative default is not the same as locking it. The lock belongs to a later design memo, which must also state explicitly that no OOS 2023+ data is in scope.

---

## 8. Focal-number posture

**The source does not single out 12.** Choosing 12 as a focal center is inherited from Candidate C's result, not from Kryon. This memo names that explicitly and refuses to let Candidate C's outcome silently become a source claim.

Two focal structures are framed here as possibilities only — neither locked:

- **Single-focal around 12.** Asks: *Does Candidate C's winning number sit in a structured neighborhood?* Directly tied to the existing result; narrowest scope.
- **Multi-focal around several centers** (e.g., 10, 12, 14, 16). Asks: *Is 12 uniquely neighborhood-structured, or does this substrate generically produce smooth neighborhoods around many focal numbers?* This is the stronger guard against mistaking generic substrate smoothness for a 12-specific effect.

The multi-focal framing is noted as the more conservative check, because a single-focal "12 looks structured" result is uninterpretable without knowing whether every center looks structured on this substrate. But focal structure is **not chosen here.** It is a design-memo lock.

---

## 9. Meaning of "neighborhood"

For Layer 1, **"neighborhood" means a linear-integer window around a focal bucket count** — nothing else.

Illustrative windows (not chosen): `{10, 11, 12, 13, 14}`, or `{8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16}`.

**Window width is a later design-memo decision.** It is not set, bounded, or hinted at here, and it must be fixed before outcomes are observed.

**Layer 1 explicitly excludes** divisor, multiple, or factor relations. Those are Layer 2. Importing any factor-based relation into Cell 1 would silently change the question from "does the source-faithful adjacency operationalization hold?" to a researcher-side extension wearing Layer 1's source authority.

---

## 10. Anti-rescue and interpretation guardrails

The following are explicitly **forbidden** in and around Cell 1:

1. Using Layer 2 to rescue a null Layer 1.
2. Using Candidate C to claim that the Kryon source specifically predicted 12.
3. Treating "12 beat 10" as proof of base-12 mathematics.
4. Changing the focal number, focal structure, or window after observing any outcome.
5. Adding divisor / multiple / factor interpretation into Cell 1 after results are known.
6. Reinterpreting Candidate B's equinox split-null as "confirmed" because Candidate C or Cell 1 is positive. The split-null stands on its own; nothing downstream confirms it retroactively.
7. Using diagnostics to promote a non-confirmatory result into a confirmatory one.

**Post-null discipline.** If Layer 1 is null: Cell 1 reports a null. Layer 2 does not automatically follow. Any continuation requires a fresh, separately argued decision memo. Curiosity about Layer 2 is legitimate; using it as an escape hatch from a Layer 1 null is not.

## 11. Candidate design directions not yet locked

Listed only to map the option space. **None of these is selected, and none implies a statistic, threshold, window, null, or verdict rule.**

- single-focal neighborhood around 12
- multi-focal neighborhood comparison across several centers
- local smoothness / peak-centeredness of the surface
- gradient or symmetry around a focal number
- comparison of the 12-neighborhood against control neighborhoods
- reuse of Candidate C's 365-anchor median PSS surface logic as possible shared infrastructure

The distinction Cell 1 must ultimately be able to draw, stated only conceptually:

- *Isolated winner:* `10 = weak, 11 = random, 12 = strong, 13 = random, 14 = weak/random`
- *Neighborhood influence:* `10 = weaker, 11 = stronger, 12 = strongest, 13 = stronger, 14 = weaker`

Cell 1's eventual job is to separate isolated-number privilege from local-surface structure. How it does so is not decided here.

## 12. Open decisions before a design memo

The following must be resolved in a later design memo, with locks fixed before any outcome is seen:

1. **Substrate lock** — confirm the frozen pullback × pooled Phase 3b substrate (the conservative default) or justify any alternative; restate OOS 2023+ exclusion.
2. **Focal structure** — single-focal (12) vs. multi-focal (e.g., 10/12/14/16), with explicit rationale.
3. **Window width and shape** — the exact linear-integer neighborhood, fixed pre-outcome.
4. **Definition of "structure"** — what observable pattern would count as neighborhood influence vs. isolated privilege vs. generic smoothness.
5. **Control / comparison design** — particularly whether and how non-focal control neighborhoods are used to rule out generic substrate smoothness.
6. **Statistic, null model, and verdict map** — none drafted here; all to be pre-registered.
7. **Infrastructure reuse decision** — whether Candidate C's surface logic is reused as-is, adapted, or rebuilt, and what that inherits.
8. **Pre-registration and pause discipline** — design memo locked and reviewed before any code, consistent with the program's phase-pause practice.

## 13. Framework-level conclusion

Influential Numbers is an umbrella program. Cell 1 — the Neighborhood Influence Test — occupies only its most source-faithful corner: linear-integer adjacency influence, the one operationalization Kryon's "four is not a four / three and five" framing directly supports.

Everything beyond adjacency — divisors, multiples, 12-family, harmonic, recursive, weighted — is Layer 2, researcher-side, and source-unauthorized. The choice of 12 as a focal center is inherited from Candidate C, not from the source, and is named as such.

Cell 1 asks a question Candidate C did not: not "does 12 beat 10?" but "is the surface around the focal number structured or is the winner isolated?" The two results are independent in both directions; neither rescues, weakens, nor reinterprets the other.

No substrate, focal structure, window, statistic, null, or verdict is locked here. The frozen Candidates B/C substrate is named only as the conservative, already-audited default, still subject to design-memo lock. The next artifact, if and when the user initiates it, is a design memo — not code, not a file, not a run.

**This memo is framework-level. It clarifies the question and its boundaries. It does not design the test.**
